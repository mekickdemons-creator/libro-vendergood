# translat

*noun, inanimate*

## Etymology

Latin *translatus* — past participle of *transferre* (to carry across; from *trans-*, across + *ferre*, to carry). Root: **translat**.

The participle form is chosen deliberately: a translat is not the act of crossing but the *result* — the standing permission to continue a journey across a boundary. In ancient Roman usage, *translatus* described goods or rights that had been officially conveyed from one party to another. In Sidis's transit vocabulary, it names the ticket that embodies that conveyance.

Related to **fidifer** (faithful carrying) and **fer** (to carry) through the *ferre* lineage, but distinct: the translat is not the carrying itself but the instrument and right that authorizes it.

## Definition

A transfer in the transit sense: the permission that allows a traveler to continue a journey on a different route or vehicle without new cost. More broadly: any instrument that encodes the right to continue a journey across a network boundary.

Sidis documented in *Notes on the Collection of Transfers* (1926) that the transit transfer is a logical object — it encodes origin, destination, time window, valid routes, and conditions of use. The translat in Vendergood carries this full meaning: not a physical ticket but the logical permission-object the ticket instantiates.

Distinguished from:

- `transit` — the act of crossing through (the journey)
- `pad` — the physical path
- `iter` — the defined route a journey follows
- **fidifer** — faithful carrying (the act of transmission); translat is what authorizes that transmission to continue

## Derivations

| Form | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **translato** | noun, inanimate | a transfer (instance; the permission-object itself) |
| **translation** | noun of act | the act of issuing or redeeming a transfer; a transit crossing |
| **translatal** | adjective | of or relating to transfers; network-crossing |
| **translatalt** | noun, habitual | one who studies, collects, or systematizes transit transfers |

Note: **translatalt** names precisely what Sidis was. The word did not exist in English or in any language he is documented to have known. He spent years being one.

## Worked sentence

**Avlo translatalt hav avlon translatan e infrat avlon rete suo.**

*The transfer-systematizer has the transfer and reasons toward its network.*

(Indicative *hav*: possesses. Intentive *infrat* — *infr-ant*: is reasoning toward, committed inference in progress. *avlon rete suo*: accusative of *rete* (network) with possessive *suo* — the network the transfer belongs to. Demonstrating the logical move Sidis made: from physical ticket to underlying graph.)
