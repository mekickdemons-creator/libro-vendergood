# orar

*verb root / noun, inanimate*

## Etymology

From Latin *hora* (hour; appointed time; the right hour, the proper moment) via the Romance path — Italian *ora*, the initial `h` dropped as in most Romance descendants. Root: **or**.

Latin *hora* carried both the unit of time and the sense of *appointed* or *fitting* time: the *hora* was not only a duration but a position in a sequence — the right hour, the hour when something is due. The Greek cognate *ὥρα* (hōra) extended this further: season, time of life, time of day, the appropriate moment. Both senses are preserved in the Vendergood root: *or* names the temporal ordering of things, not merely the measure of duration.

Phonology: root `or` is consonant-final. Nominative inanimate adds `-o`: **oro**. Suffixes beginning with a vowel attach directly with no epenthetic vowel (Vol II §4.1). Infinitive by `-ar`: **orar** — to schedule, to order by time.

## Definition

*As verb (orar):* To schedule; to arrange events in an ordered temporal sequence; to assign each thing its appointed time.

*As noun (oro):* A schedule; a timetable; the ordered temporal structure that assigns each event its position in a sequence. In transit vocabulary: the operating schedule of a network — the times at which each iter runs, at each nod. In abstract usage: any structure that assigns temporal positions to a set of events.

The *oro* is distinct from the *iter* as object: the iter is the route; the oro is the schedule that says when the iter runs. Without the iter, there is no path; without the oro, there is no time. A transit network requires both.

Distinct from:

- **temporpont** — which bridges a gap in time; the *oro* does not bridge, it orders. The oro is the structure that makes times determinate; the temporpont is the one who crosses the gap between them.
- **iter** — a defined route; the *oro* is what makes that route run at specific times.
- **nod** — a point where paths meet; the *oro* assigns arrival and departure times at each nod.

## Derivations

| Form | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **oro** | noun (inanimate, nominative) | a schedule; a timetable; an ordered temporal structure |
| **orion** | noun of act | the act of scheduling; the process of assigning temporal positions |
| **oral** | adjective | of or relating to scheduled time; temporally ordered; operating on a schedule |
| **oralt** | noun (animate, habitual) | one who schedules; a timetabler; one whose vocation is temporal ordering |
| **orant** | verb (intentive) | is scheduling; will schedule (committed future action) |
| **orar** | verb (infinitive) | to schedule; to order by time |

*Note on `oral`:* The adjective form coincides with the English word `oral` (spoken). The Vendergood `oral` means temporally ordered, not spoken — the etymologies are entirely independent (English `oral` derives from Latin *os/oris*, mouth). The Vendergood form is the correct and only derivation; the coincidence with English is superficial.

## Worked sentences

**Avlo rete vadit per avlo oro suo.**

*The network runs according to its schedule.*

(Indicative *vadit* — goes, runs. Preposition *per* — according to, by means of. Genitive *suo* — its own. The sentence names the dependency: the rete operates according to an oro; without the oro, the rete is topology without time.)

**Avlo oralt orant avlon oron iteran.**

*The scheduler is scheduling the route's timetable.*

(Animate subject with article `avlo`. Intentive *orant* — committed ongoing action. Accusative *avlon oron* — the schedule (object). Genitive adjective *iteran* — of the route. A scheduler assigning times to a specific line.)

## Compounding note

*Or-* as a modifying root in compounds: an *orter* (or + ter, "time-frame") would name a window in a schedule; an *orpont* (or + pont, "schedule-bridge") would name a transfer that depends on time alignment between routes — a timed connection. These compounds are available but not yet coined as entries.
