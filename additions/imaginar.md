# imaginar

*verb root / noun, inanimate*

## Etymology

From Latin *imago* (image; likeness; representation; mental picture) and *imaginari* (to picture to oneself; to form a mental image of). Related: *imaginatio* (imagination), *imaginarius* (imaginary, not real). Root: **imag**.

Latin *imago* was a concrete thing as well as a mental one: the wax funeral mask carried in a Roman procession, the exact likeness of the dead. It was the representation that stood *for* something absent. *Imaginari* extended this: to form such a likeness in the mind, to hold the representation of something not present. The Vendergood root preserves the key structural feature — the agent knows the thing represented is absent. This is not *videbar* (receiving an appearance as real) and not *cogitar* (holding a working belief). It is the deliberate construction of a model the agent knows does not correspond to present reality.

Phonology: root `imag` ends in a consonant. Infinitive by `-ar`: **imaginar**. The noun form `imago` is the Latin word unchanged — the Vendergood form preserves it exactly, as the meaning is also preserved exactly (Vol II §4.1). Derivational suffixes follow standard pattern (Vol II §1.3).

## Definition

*As verb (imaginar):* To project as known-false; to model something as if real while knowing it is not. The agent is not deceived — they are constructing a representation deliberately, for the purpose of reasoning about an absence, a possibility, or a hypothesis.

*As noun (imago):* An image; a projection; a mental representation held as distinct from present reality.

`Imaginar` is the fourth and final term in the Vendergood epistemic sequence:

| Term | State | The agent's relationship to the content |
|---|---|---|
| `sciar` | certain knowledge | the question is resolved; this is true |
| `cogitar` | working belief | the question is live; this may be true |
| `videbar` | uncertain perception | the thing appeared as real; truth unconfirmed |
| `imaginar` | known-false projection | the thing is not present; I am modeling it anyway |

`Imaginar` is not a failure state. It is how an agent reasons about counterfactuals, plans for absent possibilities, reconstructs past states, or extends a pattern beyond observed data. Every act of prediction involves `imaginar` — the future is not present, and the agent models it knowing this.

The final line of the Little Match Girl turns on this word: *No one imagined how beautiful the things were that she had seen.* The townspeople use `imaginar` because they cannot reach even the threshold of `videbar` — they cannot form any representation of it at all. The word names an absence of projection, not a failure of certainty.

Distinct from:

- **sciar** — the thing is known, present in the agent's settled model
- **cogitar** — the thing is believed, held as working model under genuine uncertainty
- **videbar** — the thing appeared as real; the agent received it as present
- **imaginar** — the thing is known to be absent; the agent represents it anyway

## Derivations

Derivational suffixes per Vol II §1.3. Note: `imago` (the noun) is taken directly from Latin without modification — form and meaning are identical.

| Form | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **imago** | noun (inanimate, nominative) | an image; a projection; a representation of something known absent |
| **imaginal** | adjective | imagined; projected; counterfactual; of the character of deliberate modeling |
| **imaginalt** | noun (animate, habitual) | one who projects; a modeler; one whose vocation is constructing representations of the absent |
| **imaginant** | verb (intentive) | is projecting; is modeling as known-absent (ongoing) |
| **imaginar** | verb (infinitive) | to project; to model as known-false |
| **imaginit** | verb (past) | projected; modeled; imagined |

## Worked sentences

**Nem imaginit quan belal reoi erant qui ela viderat.**

*No one imagined how beautiful the things were that she had seen.*

(From the Little Match Girl corpus. `Nem` — no one. `Imaginit` — imagined (past). `Reoi` — things (plural of `reo`, from Lat. *res*). The sentence names an absence: the townspeople could not form even a projection of what the girl had perceived. Their `imaginar` failed — the content was beyond their model space. Contrast with the girl, who `videbarit` the same things as received perceptions.)

**Avlo rete imaginar potit iter qui non esit — et per id, invenit iter qui esit.**

*The network can project a route that does not exist — and through that, find a route that does.*

(Present tense with modal `potit`. `Iter qui non esit` — a route that does not exist. The projection is explicitly counterfactual (`qui non esit`). The reason for the projection is to find an actual route: `imaginar` serves a reasoning purpose. This is the canonical AI use case: model an absent possibility in order to navigate toward a present one.)

**Ela imaginit avlo oron novon ante id esit.**

*She projected a new schedule before it existed.*

(Past `imaginit`. `Avlo oron novon` — a new schedule (object). `Ante id esit` — before it existed. The projection precedes the thing. Planning, forecasting, and design all live here: representing what is not yet present in order to bring it into being.)
