# rete

*noun, inanimate*

## Etymology

Latin *rete* (net, mesh, network). Root: **rete**.

The Latin *rete* originally named the physical fishing net — a structure of connected nodes and edges with no center. By the classical period it had extended to any interconnected system (anatomical networks, military road systems, legal webs of obligation). Sidis would have encountered it in both the literal and structural sense: the Roman road network — the greatest transit system of the ancient world — was a *rete viarum*.

## Definition

A network: a set of nodes connected by defined paths, considered as a whole system. In transit usage: the complete graph of routes, stops, and transfer points that constitute a transit system. In abstract usage: any connected structure whose behavior emerges from its topology rather than from any individual component.

The rete is not the sum of its iters. The rete is the structure that makes the iters meaningful — without the rete, an iter is just a list of places. Within the rete, an iter is a traversal of a graph.

## Derivations

| Form | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **reteo** | noun, inanimate | a network (instance; this specific system) |
| **reteion** | noun of act | the act of networking; the formation of connections |
| **reteal** | adjective | of or relating to networks; topological |
| **retealt** | noun, habitual | one who studies, designs, or maintains networks |

## Worked sentence

**Avlo rete hav avlon nod et avlon iter — avlon translatan et avlon translatan.**

*The network has nodes and routes — transfers and transfers.*

(The reduplication of *avlon translatan et avlon translatan* — "transfers and transfers" — names what a network fundamentally is: a system where movement from one iter to another is made possible and regular. The rete exists to make transfers not exceptions but infrastructure.)
