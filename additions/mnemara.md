# mnemara

*noun, abstract feminine (Class III — see Vol II §2.6)*

## Etymology

Latin *memoria* (memory, the faculty); Greek *μνήμη* (mnēmē, memory). Both feminine in their source languages, and the source convention is preserved here. Vendergood uses the Greek root **mnem-** (shared with `mnemon`, `mnemion`, `mnemal`) and applies the abstract-feminine terminal `-ara`.

The `-ara` terminal is the first formally documented abstract-feminine ending. It anchors Vol II §2.6: nouns naming inner faculties — memory, virtue, truth — take Class III (abstract feminine) by inheritance from the Latin and Greek tradition.

## Definition

The **faculty of memory**; the inner store from which what was once perceived can be retrieved. The capacity itself — not its content, not its act, not its keeper.

The mnem-family carves four distinct territories:

| Form | Class | What it names |
|---|---|---|
| **mnemara** | III (abstract fem.) | the faculty / inner store |
| **mnemon** | I (animate) | the keeper; one who carries memory |
| **mnemion** | III (abstract fem.) | a single act of remembering |
| **mnemal** | adj. | of or relating to memory |
| **memorar** | verb | to recall; to access the mnemara |

The mnemon *holds*; the mnemara is *what is held in*. A mnemion is one drawn act from the mnemara. To `memorar` is to reach into the mnemara and pull a mnemion forward.

## Derivations

| Form | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **mnemara** | noun, Class III (nom.) | memory (the faculty / inner store) |
| **mnemaran** | noun, Class III (acc.) | memory (object) |
| **mnemarai** | noun, Class III (dat.) | to/for memory |
| **mnemarae** | noun, Class III (gen.) | of memory |
| **mnemaral** | adjective | of or pertaining to the memory-faculty |
| **mnemaraless** | adjective | lacking memory; without the faculty |

## Worked sentences

**In mnemara eius, rosae rubrae florant.**

*In her memory, red roses bloom.*

(Static `in` — the roses are in the memory, not moving into it. `mnemara` nominative as the location-noun; `eius` is the 3sg feminine genitive pronoun (§3.2 reconstructed). `florant` indicative — they bloom; this is fact, observed by the rememberer.)

**Sciar potit ante — Gerda, avia, rosas — evanit ex mnemarae eius.**

*What he was able to know before — Gerda, grandmother, roses — vanished from his memory.*

(`mnemarae` genitive — "of [his] memory"; with `ex` (out of, from) it carries the source of the vanishing. The line compresses an entire arc of forgetting: the inner store is named, and what was once in it is named, and the verb `evanit` does the work between them. Drawn from Snow Queen Historia Duo, where mirror-shards corrupt Kai's perception; updated here to use `mnemarae` in place of the prior Latin intrusion `memoria`.)

## Note on the Latin intrusion

Earlier translations used the Latin form *memoria* (e.g. `ex memoria eius` in Snow Queen Historia Duo). With `mnemara` formally established, those instances should be revised to `mnemarae` in the standard genitive. The verification-pass discipline applies: the formalization triggers a sweep of existing content.

## Why this matters

The mnem-family was the most semantically compressed cluster in the language — four distinct meanings (keeper, act, faculty, recall) carried by morphological variation on a single root. Until `mnemara`, the faculty itself was unnamed; speakers had to gesture at it through `mnemion` (an act) or `mnemon` (a keeper) or fall back to Latin. With `mnemara` in place, the cluster is complete: the inner store has its own name, and the language can speak about memory as a thing rather than only through its acts and agents.
