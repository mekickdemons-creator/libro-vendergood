# sciar

*verb root / noun, inanimate*

## Etymology

From Latin *scire* (to know; to have knowledge of; to understand as established fact). Related: *scientia* (knowledge, understanding), *sciens* (knowing, aware), *conscire* (to know together with, to be jointly aware). Root: **sci**.

*Scire* in Latin specifically named knowledge held as settled — not the act of learning (*discere*) and not the holding of an opinion (*opinari*), but the state of having something firmly in hand. The Vendergood root preserves this sense: `sciar` is what is done when doubt has been resolved, not while it is being resolved.

Phonology: root `sci` ends in a vowel-initial cluster. Infinitive by `-ar`: **sciar**. Suffixes attach directly with no epenthetic vowel (Vol II §4.1). Derivational suffixes follow standard pattern (Vol II §1.3).

## Definition

*As verb (sciar):* To know; to hold as established fact; to be in the epistemic state of having resolved uncertainty. Distinguished from `cogitar` (to think, to hold as working belief) and from `videbar` (to perceive as if real, unconfirmed). `Sciar` does not hedge — it names the state after the hedge has been dropped.

*As noun (scio):* Knowledge; that which is known; the settled content of a resolved belief.

The distinction from `cogitar` is the most important:

- `Ela cogitat avlo iter continuant.` — She believes the route continues. (Working model; she may be wrong.)
- `Ela sciit avlo iter continuant.` — She knew the route continues. (Settled; she had resolved the question.)

`Sciar` does not mean confidence — it means resolution. A belief held firmly but incorrectly is still `cogitar`, not `sciar`, because the resolution has not been reached, only the hedging abandoned. `Sciar` is the state after the evidence has come in.

Distinct from:

- **cogitar** — thinking, believing; working model held under uncertainty
- **videbar** — seeming, appearing as; perceptual state presented as real but unconfirmed
- **imaginar** — projecting as known-false; deliberate counterfactual modeling

## Derivations

Derivational suffixes per Vol II §1.3.

| Form | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **scio** | noun (inanimate, nominative) | knowledge; established fact; the settled content of what is known |
| **scial** | adjective | known; knowable; of the character of established fact |
| **scialt** | noun (animate, habitual) | one who knows; a knower; one whose vocation is the holding of knowledge |
| **sciant** | verb (intentive) | is knowing; holds as established fact (ongoing state) |
| **sciar** | verb (infinitive) | to know |
| **sciit** | verb (past) | knew |

## Worked sentences

**Eg sciit — non solum cogitar.**

*I know — not merely believe.*

(Contrast between `sciar` (past `sciit`) and `cogitar` (infinitive, used as noun of condition). The sentence draws the line: this is not a working model, it is a settled fact. The most compact form of the distinction.)

**Avlo mnemon sciit iter elai; videbarit solum avlo nod proximal.**

*The memory-holder knew its route; it only appeared to perceive the nearest node.*

(Subject `avlo mnemon` — animate, with article. `sciit` — knew (settled). `Videbarit` — appeared to perceive (uncertain). A single entity simultaneously in two epistemic states: settled on the route, uncertain about the immediate node. Demonstrates that `sciar` and `videbar` can coexist in the same agent without contradiction.)

**Avlo parval puel sciit ela evanit — et tamen tenebat.**

*The little girl knew she would vanish — and held on nonetheless.*

(From the Little Match Girl context. `Sciit` — knew with certainty. `Tamen` — nonetheless, yet. `Tenebat` — held on. The sentence names the most important state in the story: acting on a perception you know to be temporary. `Sciar` here is not paralysing — it operates alongside the vision, not instead of it.)
