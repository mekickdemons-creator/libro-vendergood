# somnara

*noun, abstract feminine (Class III — see Vol II §2.6)*

## Etymology

Latin *somnium* (dream, dream-vision; the experienced content of sleep); from *somnus* (sleep). Related to Greek *ὕπνος* (hypnos, sleep) and Sanskrit *svapna* (dream) via Proto-Indo-European *\*swep-* (to sleep). The Latin distinguishes the state (*somnus*) from the content (*somnium*); Vendergood preserves the distinction by reserving the root **somn-** for the dream-content cluster.

The `-ara` terminal applies per Vol II §2.6: inner phenomena take Class III abstract feminine.

## Definition

A **dream** — the experienced content of a sleeping mind. Not the act of dreaming (a verb), not the dreamer (an agent), not the state of sleep (which is `somno`); the content itself, as if it were an object the dreamer encountered rather than authored.

Distinct from related epistemic forms:

- **cogito** — a thought (waking, voluntary or semi-voluntary)
- **imagin-** — a projection (waking, deliberate, known-false)
- **videbar** — uncertain perception (waking, but uncertain epistemic status)
- **mnemara** — the faculty of memory (the store from which content can be retrieved)

The somnara is none of these. It is *lived* by the dreamer without the dreamer's authorial control. During the dreaming, the somnara is not held as false (that would make it `imagin-`) nor merely uncertain (`videbar`) — it is simply experienced. Its epistemic status is resolved only on waking, when the dreamer steps out of the somnara's frame and looks back at it from outside.

This is why §1.5's source-channel system extends to a third dedicated form: **`somnev`** (evidential from dream-source), parallel to `memorev` and `imaginev`. Content drawn from a somnara enters discourse via `somnev` — marking that the claim's source is neither perception, report, inference, recall, nor projection, but a lived dream-sequence.

## Worked sentences

**Avla somnara per Gerdam transit dum ela dormit.**

*The dream passed through Gerda while she slept.*

(Static `per` (Vol II §2.4) — through; the dream is the moving thing, the sleeper the medium. `transit` indicative: observable from outside. `dum` simultaneous (Vol II §2.5). The sentence names the dream as a *visitor*, not a *making* — consistent with the Andersen image in Snow Queen IV where dreams pass through the dreamers like horses through a corridor.)

**Somn: avlo iter continuat, sed nullo finis venit.**

*In dream: the path continues, but no end comes.*

(Demonstrates the `somn` register particle from §1.5. The clause is flagged dream-register; individual verbs remain indicative. The assertion is true *within* the somnara's frame, not in waking reality. The compression is the point: one particle marks the entire clause's epistemic mode, no per-verb marking needed.)

**Avlo facalt non sciat utrum vidat an somnit.**

*The match-bearer does not know whether he sees or dreams.*

(The classical boundary problem. `sciat` indicative negative — does not know; `utrum...an...` Latin alternative construction; `vidat` (sees) and `somnit` (dreams) as the two epistemic states between which resolution is unavailable. The sentence is in waking-frame: the speaker, looking at the facalt from outside, names the indeterminacy. From inside the somnara, this question cannot be asked.)

## Note

Anchors the dream-register extension to Vol II §1.5. The `-ara` terminal continues the productive pattern established by `mnemara`, `glorara`, and `animara`. The four together form a taxonomy of inner phenomena:

- **mnemara** — faculty (the store)
- **glorara** — standing (the position)
- **animara** — principle (what is alive)
- **somnara** — content-from-sleep (what is lived without authoring)

Each is Class III abstract feminine; each takes `avla` as article; each declines on the vowel-final feminine paradigm. The pattern is now stable enough that further Class III coinings in `-ara` should follow without ceremony.
