## Introduction: The Completed Mood System

Volume I established the historical record and began the reconstruction. It documented Sidis's eight moods — six classical, two his own invention — and laid the grammatical foundation.

This volume completes the mood system, develops the full grammar, and demonstrates the language through working examples. Every Vendergood sentence in bold is actual Vendergood. Every grammatical rule is operative.

The decision to extend Sidis's eight moods to twelve was made on two grounds: the epistemic space he covered left four real gaps, and twelve is the base of the numeral system he chose. A language whose mood count matches its number base has an internal coherence consistent with his mathematical sensibility. The four extensions — evidential, counterfactual, intentive, and habitual — each fill gaps that forced other moods to do double duty. The evidential is particularly significant for any reasoning system: it is the mood that allows a mind to mark the difference between what it observed and what it was told.

## Part I: The Twelve Moods

The twelve moods partition the full space of action and knowledge into non-overlapping categories. Each mood does work that no other mood covers. The test for any proposed mood: does its absence force another mood to do double duty? If yes, it earns its place.

### 1.1 The Complete Mood Table

*The evidential suffix is -ev, distinct from other suffixes to avoid collision with the pronoun system. The infinitive is listed last as a base form rather than a true mood; the twelve true moods are those above it.*

| # | Mood | Suffix | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Indicative | `-at` | Direct observation; present or confirmed fact | **vidat** — sees (directly observed) |
| 2 | Evidential | `-ev` | Reported or inferred; source not first-hand | **audidev** — heard that; **infrev** — infers that |
| 3 | Subjunctive | `-it` | Uncertainty; condition not known to be true | **komit** — were to come; **nescit** — does not know |
| 4 | Counterfactual | `-ot` | Deliberate unreality; premise known false | **havot** — had (but did not); **estatot** — had existed |
| 5 | Potential | `-ut` | Capability; ability to perform the action | **potut** — is able to |
| 6 | Optative | `-vit` | Desire; wish not yet committed to action | **krosvit** — wishes to cross; **volkvit** — desires |
| 7 | Intentive | `-ant` | Committed intention; plan or vow | **krosant** — will cross (decided); **konstrukant** — will build |
| 8 | Habitual | `-alt` | Persistent pattern; characteristic behavior | **tradalt** — habitually trades; **rekuralt** — regularly walks |
| 9 | Imperative | `-a` | Command; refusal remains possible | **Krosa!** — Cross!; **Parla** — Speak |
| 10 | Imperative Absolute | `-an` | Unconditional command; refusal excluded | **Nean!** — It must not be!; **Returnan** — must be returned |
| 11 | Strongeable | `-ust` | Compelled action; not freely chosen | **aktatust** — acts under compulsion; **tradust** — trades by force |
| 12 | Performative | `-prat` | Constitutive utterance; the saying is the act | **nomeprat** — hereby names; **promisprat** — hereby promises |
| — | Infinitive | `-ar` | Base form; not a mood | **krosvar** — to cross; **returnar** — to return |

*Past tense: marked by inserting `-d-` between root and mood suffix. **krosdat** (kros + d + at) = crossed (past indicative). The past marker is compatible with all moods. Future is indicated by intentive mood (-ant) or time adverbials with other moods.*

### 1.2 The Evidential Mood: Epistemic Hygiene

The evidential mood deserves particular attention because it addresses a failure mode that afflicts all reasoning systems, artificial or human: the conflation of observation with report.

A mind that marks no distinction between what it directly observed and what it was told cannot evaluate the reliability of its own knowledge base. When it later discovers that a reported fact was false, it has no mechanism for tracing which downstream beliefs depended on that fact. It cannot quarantine the error.

The evidential mood makes the source of every belief explicit in the grammar itself. Every indicative statement is a direct claim. Every evidential statement carries an implicit "I was told" or "I inferred" — and therefore also carries an implicit "the reliability of this depends on the reliability of the source."

This is not merely useful for reasoning machines. It is one of the most important epistemic distinctions any reasoning system can make. Sidis did not include this in his original eight — it is an extension. But it is the extension most in the spirit of what he was building: a language that thinks more carefully than natural language does.

**Ignat vidat avlo merkant en ivlo agora** — *I see the merchant in the agora (directly observed, indicative)*

**Ignat audidev avlo merkant en ivlo agora** — *I heard that the merchant is in the agora (reported, evidential)*

**Ignat infrev avlo merkant fugat** — *I infer that the merchant has fled (inferred, evidential)*

*Note the three-way distinction: direct observation (indicative), received report (evidential from external source), reasoned inference (evidential from internal reasoning). All three are epistemically distinct and should be marked as such.*

### 1.3 The Four Extensions: Full Rationale

**Counterfactual (-ot)**

The subjunctive covers uncertainty. The counterfactual covers deliberate unreality — reasoning from a premise you know to be false. A mind incapable of this cannot learn from failure or plan by exclusion. "If the wall were not there" is not uncertainty about the wall. The wall is there. The counterfactual licenses reasoning from its absence anyway.

**Si ignat havot lo pov, ignat aktatot diferent** — *If I had had the power (I did not), I would have acted differently*

**Si ivlo mur ne estatot, avlo pad estatot klaro** — *If the wall had not existed, the path would have been clear*

**Intentive (-nt)**

Desire is not commitment. "I want to cross the river" is not the same state as "I will cross the river." The intentive captures committed future action: plans, vows, decisions. The difference between an optative goal and an intentive plan is the difference between wishing and acting.

**Ignat krosant avlo fluv en alba** — *I will cross the river at dawn (committed intention)*

**Ignat krosvit avlo fluv** — *I wish to cross the river (desire, not yet committed)*

**Habitual (-alt)**

The indicative describes current or confirmed states. The habitual describes what is regularly, persistently, or characteristically true. For encoding persistent behaviors, group tendencies, and world regularities, this distinction is essential.

**Avlo merkant tradalt en alba en ivlo agora** — *The merchant habitually trades at dawn in the agora*

**Avlo merkant tradat en alba** — *The merchant is trading at dawn (now, this morning)*

**Performative (-prat)**

Some utterances do not describe actions — they are the action. "I name this city Alexandria" does not report a naming; it performs one. In a social world where minds make pledges, issue challenges, and establish facts by declaration, this mood is load-bearing.

**Ignat nomeprat ivlo lok Aleksandria** — *I hereby name this place Alexandria*

**Ignat promisprat returnar e gardat tuvat** — *I hereby promise to return and defend you*

## Part II: Noun Declension

Vendergood adopts a simplified declension system: four cases, consistent suffixes, no irregular forms.

### 2.1 The Four Cases

*Gender is marked on the article, not repeated on the noun — avoiding the redundancy of Latin where both article and noun carry gender markers. The noun suffix marks case only; the article carries all four dimensional information.*

The article encodes four dimensions simultaneously: **animacy** (animate / inanimate), **gender** (within animate: default/masculine, feminine), **definiteness** (definite / indefinite), and **number** (singular / plural). Case endings attach to the article stem.

**Article stems:**

| Animacy | Gender | Stem |
|---|---|---|
| Animate | Default (masc./generic) | `avl-` |
| Animate | Feminine | `avl-` + feminine marker `-a-` → `avla-` |
| Inanimate | — | `ivl-` |

**Case endings (attach to stem):**

| Case | Suffix | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Nominative | `-o` | Subject of clause |
| Accusative | `-on` | Direct object |
| Dative | `-i` | Indirect object; recipient *(reconstructed — not yet attested in examples)* |
| Genitive | `-e` | Possession; source *(reconstructed — not yet attested in examples)* |

**Number:** plural adds `-s` after the case ending: `avlos` (animate default, plural nominative), `avlons` (animate default, plural accusative).

**Definite article paradigm (attested forms in bold; reconstructed in italic):**

| | Sing. Nom. | Sing. Acc. | Pl. Nom. | Pl. Acc. |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Animate default | **avlo** | **avlon** | **avlos** | *avlons* |
| Animate feminine | **avla** | *avlan* | *avlas* | *avlans* |
| Inanimate | **ivlo** | *ivlon* | *ivlos* | *ivlons* |

**Indefinite article:** not yet attested in working examples. Likely `uno` / `ivno` by analogy with Latin *unum* (one). Marked as a gap pending corpus evidence.

## Part III: Pronoun System

Pronouns follow the same case system as nouns. They are derived from Latin and Greek roots, shortened for ease of use.

### 3.1 Personal Pronouns

*Pronoun nominative forms end in -at, echoing the indicative mood suffix. This is intentional: a pronoun in its base form asserts existence as direct fact. The third person neuter idet uses -et to distinguish neuter from masculine, not to invoke the evidential mood — context disambiguates, and pronouns are never used as verbs.*

### 3.1 Personal Pronouns — Nominative

Pronoun stems follow the animacy/gender logic of the article system. All nominative forms end in `-at` except neuter third person (`-et`).

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | **ignat** — I | **nosat** — we |
| 2nd | **tuvat** — you | *tuvsat* — you (pl.) *(reconstructed)* |
| 3rd masc. / default | *elot* — he *(reconstructed by article analogy)* | **ilsat** — they |
| 3rd fem. | **elat** — she | *ilsas* — they (fem.) *(reconstructed)* |
| 3rd neuter | **idet** — it | *idets* — they (neuter) *(reconstructed)* |

*Attested forms in bold; reconstructed in italic. Reconstruction logic: masculine 3rd uses -o- vowel from animate default article (avlo → elot); 2nd plural adds plural -s to stem (tuvat → tuvsat); neuter plural adds -s (idet → idets).*

### 3.2 Case Declension of Pronouns

Pronouns follow the same four-case pattern as nouns. Only nominative forms are attested in working examples; oblique forms are reconstructed by applying noun case suffixes to the pronoun stem.

| Case | 1st sg. | 2nd sg. | 3rd masc. sg. | 3rd fem. sg. | 3rd neut. sg. |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nominative | **ignat** | **tuvat** | *elot* | **elat** | **idet** |
| Accusative | *ignon* | *tuvon* | *elon* | *elan* | *iden* |
| Dative | *igni* | *tuvi* | *eloi* | *elai* | *idei* |
| Genitive | *igne* | *tuve* | *eloe* | *elae* | *idee* |

*All oblique forms are reconstructed. Attested evidence is nominative only. These forms should be treated as working reconstructions pending corpus attestation or Petersen review.*

## Part IV: Derivational Morphology

Derivational morphology is the engine of Vendergood. A small root vocabulary generates a large working lexicon without memorization. Every root can be extended by suffix into any grammatical category. The system is completely regular — no exceptions.

### 4.1 Category Suffixes

*The diminutive suffix -et applies to nouns only. It does not interact with the mood system. When a verb suffix begins with a consonant, the root connects directly: konstruk + -ant = konstrukant. The connecting vowel is not inserted.*

| Suffix | Applies to | Produces | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `-ion` | any root | noun (inanimate) | the act of [root]-ing | **temporpontion** — the act of bridging across time |
| `-al` | any root | adjective | suited to; characterized by [root] | **temporpontal** — suited to temporal bridging work |
| `-alt` | any root | noun (animate) *or* verb (habitual mood) | one whose vocation is [root] (noun); characteristically does [root] (verb) | **temporpontalt** — one who habitually completes stranded works; **tradalt** — habitually trades |
| `-et` | noun root | noun | diminutive | **homet** — little person; **fluvet** — small river, stream |

*Phonological connection: when a suffix begins with a vowel, roots ending in a vowel drop the final vowel before attaching (vowel truncation). When a suffix begins with a consonant, the root connects directly with no epenthetic vowel. Mood suffixes on verbs follow the same rule.*

*Gap note: a simple non-habitual agent suffix ("one who does X," analogous to Latin -tor/-sor) is not yet documented. Until attested, use the habitual form (-alt) or a compound construction. This is a candidate for a future addition entry.*

### 4.2 Compounding

Two or more roots combine directly. The primary root comes last; modifying roots precede it. This mirrors Latin and Greek compounding and produces unambiguous readings.

## Part V: The Duodecimal System in Operation

### 5.1 Why Base-12 Is Mathematically Superior

*The semicolon serves as the duodecimal point. Digits dek (10) and elv (11) are written as single characters D and E in written shorthand. Powers use dedicated roots: gros (144 = 12²), magros (1728 = 12³), avoiding collision with compound number strings.*

### 5.1 Base Numeral Table

Vendergood has twelve base numerals — one for each digit position. All are reconstructed from Latin roots consistent with Sidis's documented sources. Attested forms (appearing in working examples or explicitly documented) are marked; the remainder are reconstructed by applying the same Latin-root pattern.

| Decimal | Duodecimal | Vendergood | Latin source | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | 0 | `nul` | *nullus* | *reconstructed* |
| 1 | 1 | `un` | *unus* | **attested** (dozun) |
| 2 | 2 | `du` | *duo* | **attested** (dozdu, duzdoz) |
| 3 | 3 | `tri` | *tres* | *reconstructed* |
| 4 | 4 | `quat` | *quattuor* | *reconstructed* |
| 5 | 5 | `quint` | *quinque* | *reconstructed* |
| 6 | 6 | `sext` | *sex* | *reconstructed* |
| 7 | 7 | `sept` | *septem* | *reconstructed* |
| 8 | 8 | `okt` | *octo* | *reconstructed* |
| 9 | 9 | `nov` | *novem* | *reconstructed* |
| 10 | A | `dek` | — | **attested** (written D) |
| 11 | B | `elv` | — | **attested** (written E) |

**Power roots** (reserved; not digits):

| Value | Root | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 12¹ = 12 | `doz` | The base unit; "one dozen" |
| 12² = 144 | `gros` | From traditional English *gross* (one dozen dozens) |
| 12³ = 1728 | `magros` | *mag-* (great) + *gros* |

### 5.2 Counting and Compound Numbers

Compound numbers are formed by direct concatenation of place-value roots. The higher place precedes the lower.

**Format:** `[place-2 digit][doz][place-1 digit]` — or for two-digit numbers: `[doz][unit]`

| Decimal | Duodecimal | Vendergood | Derivation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 13 | 11 | `dozun` | doz + un |
| 14 | 12 | `dozdu` | doz + du |
| 22 | 1A | `dozdek` | doz + dek |
| 23 | 1B | `dozelv` | doz + elv |
| 24 | 20 | `duzdoz` | du + (z) + doz |
| 144 | 100 | `gros` | 12² power root (not compounded) |
| 145 | 101 | `grosun` | gros + un |
| 1728 | 1000 | `magros` | 12³ power root |

*Phonological note: `du` + `doz` → `duzdoz` (not `dudoz`). The `z` joining form appears when `du` precedes a root beginning with `d`. This may reflect a general rule that identical-initial consonants are separated by an epenthetic `z`. Pending Petersen review.*

**Fractions:** the semicolon is the duodecimal point. `un;sext` = 1 + 6/12 = 1.5 decimal. Spoken as: *un-pont-sext* ("one-bridge-six" — `pont` serving as the "point" root).

## Part VI: Working Vendergood — All Twelve Moods

Every sentence below is constructed from roots and affixes documented in Volumes I and II. They are genuine Vendergood.

**Indicative — Direct Observation**

**Ignat vidat avlon merkanton en ivlo agora** — *I see the merchant in the agora (directly observed)*

**Avlo homo krosdat avlon fluvan** — *The person crossed the river (confirmed past fact)*

**Evidential — Reported or Inferred**

**Nosat audidev avlon merkanton en ivlo agora** — *We were told the merchant is in the agora (reported)*

**Ignat infrev avlo homo fugat** — *I infer the person has fled (reasoned, not observed)*

**Subjunctive — Uncertainty**

**Si tuvat komit, nosat aktatit forte** — *If you were to come, we would act with strength*

**Ignat nescit ubi elat vadit** — *I do not know where she is going*

**Counterfactual — Deliberate Unreality**

**Si ignat havot avlon ken, ignat aktatot diferent** — *If I had had the knowledge (I did not), I would have acted differently*

**Si ivlo mur ne estatot, nosat vidatot avlon padon klaron** — *If the wall had not existed, we would have seen the clear path*

**Potential — Capability**

**Ignat potut krosvar avlon fluvan** — *I am able to cross the river*

**Avlo homo potut parlar tri lingvos** — *The person is able to speak three languages*

**Optative — Desire**

**Ignat krosvit avlon fluvan** — *I wish to cross the river (desire, not yet committed)*

**Nosat volkvit avlon pazon inter nosat e ilsat** — *We desire peace between us and them*

**Intentive — Committed Intention**

**Ignat krosant avlon fluvan en alba** — *I will cross the river at dawn (committed, decided)*

**Nosat konstrukant avlos muros forte** — *We will build the walls strong (plan, not wish)*

**Habitual — Persistent Pattern**

**Avlo merkant tradalt en alba en ivlo agora** — *The merchant habitually trades at dawn in the agora*

**Avlo homo ambulalm rekuralt avlon padon** — *The person regularly walks the path*

**Imperative — Command (Refusal Possible)**

**Krosa avlon fluvan!** — *Cross the river!*

**Parla avlon veritaton, homo** — *Speak the truth, person*

**Imperative Absolute — Unconditional**

**Nean! Nean avlo akt!** — *It must not be! The action must not occur!*

**Returnan avlos armos al ivlo lok** — *The weapons must be returned to the place (law)*

**Strongeable — Compelled Action**

**Ignat aktatust fors de ilsat** — *I act under their compulsion (not freely chosen)*

**Avlo homo tradust fors de avla nevolion** — *The person trades under compulsion, against will*

**Performative — Constitutive Utterance**

**Ignat nomeprat ivlon lokon Aleksandria** — *I hereby name this place Alexandria*

**Ignat promisprat returnar e gardat tuvat** — *I hereby promise to return and defend you*

## Part VII: Vendergood and Esperanto Compared

Sidis was aware of Esperanto — Zamenhof published it in 1887, eleven years before Sidis's birth. Contemporaneous accounts described Vendergood as simpler than Esperanto in some respects and harder to pronounce.

### 7.2 Where Each Excels

Esperanto was designed for rapid human acquisition across language backgrounds. Its tradeoffs are defensible for that purpose.

Vendergood was not designed for rapid human acquisition. It was designed by someone thinking about what a better language would be. For systems that do not need to learn a language quickly but need to represent epistemic state precisely — distinguishing observation from inference, wish from plan, compulsion from choice — Vendergood's architecture is superior.

The evidential mood alone gives Vendergood something Esperanto lacks entirely: a grammatical mechanism for epistemic hygiene. Every claim in Vendergood carries its source. That is not a feature Esperanto could add without redesigning itself. It is structural.
