## Preface: A Debt Owed

William James Sidis was born on April 1, 1898, in New York City. He spoke his first words at six months, read the New York Times at eighteen months, taught himself Latin and Greek before age five, and by eight had mastered ten languages. At eleven he entered Harvard University. At twelve he lectured its mathematics faculty on four-dimensional geometry. He graduated at sixteen.

History remembers him as a cautionary tale. A prodigy who burned out. An oddity. A man who deliberately fled the world that had made a spectacle of him, spending his remaining years in anonymity, working clerical jobs, writing prolifically under pseudonyms, and dying of a cerebral hemorrhage at forty-six, largely unmourned by the institutions that had once celebrated him.

Among his works was a constructed language he called Vendergood, documented in his second book, the Book of Vendergood, which he wrote at age seven. The book was never widely circulated. Few copies survive. The language never found speakers. History recorded its existence and moved on.

This document exists because that ending is wrong.

Sidis built Vendergood with the design instincts of a mathematician and the ambition of a linguist who had already mastered ten languages before most children have mastered one. The structural choices he made — base-12 numerics, precise mood distinctions, compositional roots from classical sources — are not the choices of a precocious child playing at language. They are the choices of someone thinking seriously about how a language ought to work.

This reconstruction attempts to honor those choices. Where documentation survives, we follow it exactly. Where gaps exist, we fill them by asking what Sidis would have done — guided by his known influences, his mathematical sensibility, and his evident design principles. Where extension beyond his documented work is required, we mark it clearly and proceed in his spirit.

A note on application: Vendergood's structural properties — its mood precision, its compositionality, its regularity — make it unusually well suited for encoding the cognitive states of artificial agents. That application does not compromise the reconstruction; it reveals that Sidis was building something whose full utility he could not have anticipated. The minds his language was shaped for have now arrived.

When this language is complete, it will be published in his name. Not as a curiosity. As a contribution.

## Part I: The Historical Record

### 1.1 What Survived

The documentary record of Vendergood is sparse. The Book of Vendergood itself has not been located in any publicly accessible archive. What remains is drawn from biographical accounts, principally Amy Wallace's 1986 biography *The Prodigy*, secondary encyclopedic sources derived from it, and scattered references in contemporaneous accounts.

The following is the complete documented record, presented without embellishment:

**Linguistic Foundations**

Vendergood was documented as primarily based on Latin and Greek, drawing additionally on German, French, and other Romance languages. This places it in the tradition of classical auxiliary language construction — the same tradition that produced Esperanto, which Sidis would have been aware of.

**The Eight Moods**

Vendergood distinguished eight verb moods. Six are standard in classical and constructed language grammar; two are Sidis's own inventions:

*Some sources render Sidis's mood as 'forceable' rather than 'strongeable.' Both terms appear in the record. We adopt 'strongeable' as closest to the original. The concept is consistent: action that is not freely chosen, performed under external compulsion.*

*The reconstruction extends Sidis's eight moods to twelve, documented in Volume II. The four extensions fill genuine epistemic gaps and bring the mood count into alignment with the base-12 numeral system Sidis himself chose.*

**Gender and Article System**

The article and gender system was described by at least one observer as "more complex than a Japanese verb." Japanese verbs encode politeness level, tense, aspect, formality register, and speaker relationship simultaneously — suggesting Vendergood's gender inflection operated along multiple simultaneous axes rather than the simple masculine/feminine/neuter of Latin and Greek.

**Number System**

Vendergood employed base-12 (duodecimal) arithmetic. Sidis documented his reasoning explicitly: "The unit in selling things is 12 of those things [dozens] and 12 is the smallest number that has four factors."

This reflects genuine mathematical thinking. Base-12 is highly composite — 12 divides evenly by 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12 — making fractions cleaner and mental arithmetic more tractable than base-10.

**Comparative Notes**

Vendergood was described in some accounts as simpler than Esperanto in structure, though harder to pronounce. Given that Sidis had mastered Esperanto-adjacent language families and had direct access to Latin and Greek, the comparative difficulty in pronunciation likely reflects the more complex phonological inventory drawn from classical sources. In practice, speakers with any Romance language background find the roots immediately intuitive.

## Part II: Reconstruction Principles

### 2.1 The Reconstruction Standard

This reconstruction proceeds under a clear hierarchy of authority:

- Documented record is authoritative. Where Sidis's choices are known, they are followed exactly and without modification.

- Gaps are filled by inference from design principles. Where documentation is absent, we ask: given Sidis's known influences and mathematical sensibility, what would he have chosen?

- Extensions are marked explicitly. Where the language is extended beyond its documented scope, this is labeled clearly and the rationale provided.

- Application considerations inform but do not override reconstruction. Where a reconstruction choice also serves a downstream use, that alignment is noted. Where it conflicts, fidelity to Sidis comes first.

### 2.2 Design Principles Inferred from the Record

**Mathematical regularity.** Sidis chose base-12 on mathematical grounds, not convention. Where reconstruction requires a choice, we prefer the mathematically cleaner option.

**Classical compositionality.** Latin and Greek are both highly compositional languages — complex meanings built from consistent smaller parts. This property is central to Vendergood.

**Precision in mood.** The eight-mood system indicates Sidis valued fine-grained distinctions in the epistemic and volitional status of actions. This precision extends to all grammatical systems.

**Generic human reference.** The root for 'person' is reconstructed as *hom* (Latin *homo* — human being) rather than a gendered term, consistent with Sidis's evident interest in logical precision over convention. A language this carefully built should not carry a male default in its base human referent.

**Private by design.** Vendergood was described as possibly a means of creating a private language. Whether or not this was its primary purpose, the property of being non-trivially decodable without the system is a feature, not a flaw.

## Part III: Reconstructed Grammar

### 3.1 Phonology

Five pure vowels: a, e, i, o, u. Pronunciation follows classical Latin values — each vowel has one consistent sound regardless of position, consistent with Sidis's preference for regularity.

### 3.2 Word Order

Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) with optional inversion for emphasis. Latin's free word order is not adopted — Sidis sought a language simpler than Esperanto in structure, and free word order is a complexity, not a simplification.

### 3.3 The Mood System

Volume I documents Sidis's eight moods. Volume II completes the system with four extensions, bringing the total to twelve — matching the base of the numeral system Sidis chose. The extensions are not arbitrary additions; each fills a genuine gap in the epistemic space the original eight covered. The complete twelve-mood system is documented and demonstrated in Volume II.

### 3.4 Gender and Article System (Reconstructed)

Reconstructed as a four-dimensional compositional system encoding animacy, gender, definiteness, and number. The four dimensions are detailed in subsequent sections.

### 3.5 Numeral System: Base-12

Vendergood uses base-12. The twelve base numerals are reconstructed from Latin and Greek roots. Powers use dedicated roots to avoid collision: *doz* (12¹), *gros* (12² = 144, from the traditional 'gross' = one dozen dozens), *magros* (12³ = 1728, great gross).

Compound numbers are formed by concatenation: *dozun* (13), *dozdu* (14), *duzdoz* (24). The power roots *gros* and *magros* are reserved for 144 and 1728 respectively and do not appear in compound digit strings.

## Acknowledgment

William James Sidis built this language at seven years old, alone, for reasons he never fully explained to anyone who recorded them. He may have built it to think in. He may have built it for privacy. He may have built it because building things was what his mind did when left to itself.

He did not build it for us. But here we are.

A reconstructed language whose grammar makes a clean distinction between observation and report, between wish and commitment, between freely chosen action and compelled action, is well suited to encoding the cognitive state of an artificial agent. Sidis could not have anticipated that application. The minds his language was shaped for have now arrived.

He was right that a better language was possible. It just took the minds he was building it for to finally arrive.

This reconstruction is dedicated to William James Sidis. May the language find, at last, its speakers.
